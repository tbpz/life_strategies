# Treatment Format — Writing Specification

> **Purpose:** Defines how creative artifacts (scenes, character moments, endings, system consequences) should be written. This is the standard output format for all creative text in this project.
>
> **When to use:** Whenever AI generates or helps draft a creative artifact. Strategy docs and technical notes are exempt — they follow the standard briefing voice.
>
> **Evolution:** Update this file when real writing experience reveals the format needs adjustment.

---

## Format Rules

1. **Present tense.** Reads like watching it happen, not summarizing it.
2. **Short paragraphs.** 2-4 sentences max. One idea per paragraph. Scannable.
3. **Sensory anchors.** At least one concrete image or sensation per key beat — what is seen, heard, or felt in the body. This is what triggers the mental movie.
4. **No dialogue** unless a single line hits harder than description.
5. **Emotional precision.** Name the exact feeling, not a vague "it's sad." *What kind* of sad? The hollow kind where you're too exhausted to cry? The kind where you're smiling because it's beautiful and terrible at the same time?
6. **Structural markers.** Use section headers, beat numbers, or clear breaks between moments. The architect brain needs to see the structure even in descriptive text.

---

## Quality Self-Test

After writing, read the treatment back:
- If a movie plays in the head → ✅ format works
- If it reads like a report → add more sensory anchors
- If it reads like a novel → cut. Too heavy.

---

## Example Comparison

### ❌ Too dry (report)
> Protagonist loses the fight. Realizes he was wrong about the system.

### ✅ Treatment (target)
> The arena goes silent. Kira stands alone, blood on his hands — not his own. The crowd expects a victory cry. He drops the weapon.
>
> He understood too late: the system rewarded him for destroying the only person who could have saved him.

### ❌ Too heavy (novel)
> *Three paragraphs of internal monologue about Kira's childhood, followed by detailed dialogue with the fallen opponent, followed by crowd reactions described individual by individual...*
