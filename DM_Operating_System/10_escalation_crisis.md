# Function 10: Escalation & Crisis Management
*When and how to raise problems to leadership*

**Principle:** Escalation is not failure — it is judgment. The DM who never escalates is absorbing problems that leadership needs to know about. The DM who escalates everything is abdicating decision-making. The skill is calibration.

---

## Method

*At Project Start — Define Thresholds:*
Document the escalation thresholds once, at project start:

| Problem Type | Escalate to | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Schedule slip > [X] days | PM + Line Manager | Immediately when trajectory is clear |
| Budget at risk > [X]% | Line Manager + Finance | As soon as trajectory is visible |
| Team conflict affecting delivery | Line Manager / HR | After one direct resolution attempt fails |
| Client relationship risk | Line Manager | Immediately — before client escalates themselves |
| Delivery problem (execution) | DM owns | Attempt resolution; escalate if unresolved in 48h |
| Strategic problem (contract, resourcing, direction) | Leadership owns | Escalate immediately with framing |

*Delivery vs. Strategic Problem:*
- **Delivery problem (DM owns):** Blocked task, missed sprint, quality issue, team coordination failure, scope ambiguity
- **Strategic problem (leadership owns):** Contract scope dispute, resourcing decision above DM authority, relationship with client deteriorating, business direction changing mid-project

When in doubt: escalate. Over-escalating in the early weeks of a new environment is less costly than under-escalating.

*Escalation Format:*
Never escalate with just "we have a problem." Always bring:
1. **Problem statement** — what is happening
2. **Impact** — what it affects (timeline, budget, client relationship)
3. **Options** — what has been considered (2–3 options minimum)
4. **Recommendation** — which option is preferred and why

The goal is to reduce the decision-maker's cognitive load, not to hand the problem to them.

*Escalation Timing Rule:*
If a problem will still exist in 24 hours regardless of individual effort — escalate now, not after the analysis is perfect. A good-enough brief delivered early is more valuable than a perfect brief delivered late.

*Crisis Response Sequence:*
1. **Contain** — stop the bleeding; prevent the problem from expanding
2. **Communicate** — inform stakeholders with facts, not speculation; state what is known and what is being investigated
3. **Investigate** — establish root cause
4. **Remediate** — resolve the immediate problem
5. **Prevent recurrence** — brief retrospective, system fix, not blame

*Post-Escalation Retrospective:*
After any major escalation or crisis: hold a 30-minute retrospective. Not to find blame, but to answer: "What in our process allowed this to happen, and what can be changed?"

---

## Cadence

Escalation thresholds defined at project start. Retrospective held within one week of any major escalation.

---

## Artifact

Escalation thresholds document (one table, project start). Escalation note (4-point format above). Post-escalation retrospective notes.

---

## Adaptation Notes

| Context | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| US/EU client-facing | Client must hear bad news from DM directly, before hearing from internal sources |
| Outsourcing firm (internal) | Both client-facing and internal escalation paths must be clearly defined |
| Small team / flat hierarchy | Escalation path is shorter; DM may escalate directly to CEO/CTO |
| Remote/async team | Crisis escalation cannot wait for a meeting; synchronous channel needed immediately |

---

## Self-Check

- Are escalation thresholds defined for this project?
- Is there any problem currently being held back that leadership would want to know about?
- Has any escalation taken more than 48 hours from problem identification to communication?

---

## Gap to Close

Apply the 24h rule consistently: if a problem will still exist tomorrow, communicate it today. The thoroughness of the analysis matters less than the timeliness of the alert.
