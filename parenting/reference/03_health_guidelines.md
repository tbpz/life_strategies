# Health & Physical Development — Guidelines Reference

This file contains WHO/CDC/AAP evidence-based guidelines for physical activity, sleep, screen time, and nutrition. Use as a reference when specific numbers are needed.

---

## Physical Activity & Motor Development

**Why it matters beyond fitness:** Physical play is the primary vehicle for cognitive development in the first 3 years. Crawling builds spatial awareness. Reaching and grasping build neural pathways. Outdoor exploration develops sensory processing. Physical development *is* brain development.

| Age | WHO Physical Activity Guideline | Practical Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Infant (<1y)** | Multiple times daily; ≥30 min tummy time per day | Floor time, reaching games, tummy time, crawling practice |
| **Toddler (1–2y)** | ≥180 min total activity per day (any intensity) | Walking, climbing, outdoor exploration, park time |
| **Pre-school (3–4y)** | ≥180 min including ≥60 min moderate-to-vigorous | Running, jumping, playground, swimming, bike |
| **School age (5+)** | ≥60 min moderate-to-vigorous daily | Sports, active play, family outdoor activities |

**Key principle:** Young children should not be restrained (stroller, car seat, high chair) for more than 1 hour at a time.

## Sleep

Sleep is not optional — it is when the brain consolidates learning and growth hormone is released. Sleep deprivation at this age has disproportionately large cognitive effects.

| Age | Recommended Sleep (per 24h) | Key Actions |
|---|---|---|
| **0–3 months** | 14–17 hours | On-demand; establish early patterns |
| **4–11 months** | 12–16 hours | Consistent bedtime routine; same time daily |
| **1–2 years** | 11–14 hours | Include naps; screen-free before bed |
| **3–5 years** | 10–13 hours | Bedtime ritual (brush, book, bed) |
| **6+ years** | 9–12 hours | Protect bedtime from homework/screens |

**Bedtime ritual formula:** Same time → same sequence → wind-down activity (bath, book) → sleep. Consistency matters more than the specific activities chosen.

## Screen Time

Evidence is unambiguous — especially for under-2. The "video deficit effect": infants and toddlers learn from real-world interaction; screen-based content displaces serve-and-return interactions that build the brain.

| Age | WHO/AAP Guideline | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Under 18 months** | **No screen time** (except video calls) | Screens displace real-world interaction; learning transfer is near-zero |
| **18–24 months** | Minimal, high-quality, co-viewed only | If used, watch together and narrate/discuss |
| **2–5 years** | ≤1 hour/day of high-quality content | Quality matters more than duration; co-viewing preferred |
| **6+ years** | Consistent limits; screen-free zones | Ensure screens don't displace sleep, physical activity, or face-to-face interaction |

**Non-negotiable rules for all ages:**
- Screen-free bedrooms
- Screen-free mealtimes (family meals are social-emotional development time)
- No screens as a default soothing tool (teaches the child that screens = emotion management)

## Nutrition

Strategic principles only — not a diet plan:
- Follow pediatrician guidance for age-appropriate feeding transitions
- Introduce diverse foods early (before 12 months) to reduce pickiness later
- Family meals together when possible — simultaneous social-emotional development
- Avoid using food as reward or punishment (distorts the child's relationship with food)
- Model healthy eating — children copy parental habits, not parental instructions

## Medical & Safety

- Follow the Vietnamese national immunization schedule completely
- Regular pediatric checkups — use these proactively to raise developmental concerns
- Childproof the home environment as mobility increases (crawling → walking → climbing)
- Download the CDC Milestone Tracker app — free, evidence-based, age-specific checklists
- **Trust instinct:** if something seems off developmentally, raise it with a pediatrician early. Early intervention is significantly more effective than late intervention.
